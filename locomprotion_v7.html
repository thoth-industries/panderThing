Rural Co-Promotion as Cultural and Economic Strategy
On the Art of Small Towns Becoming Themselves
When local businesses promote each other instead of competing, they stop being isolated shops and start being a place — and people will drive an hour to experience a place in a way they will never drive an hour for a shop. This paper asks what that simple observation is worth, and to whom.
American Christian Mind Project · Lewisburg, Kentucky · 2026 · Seasonal Edition

There is a word that does not yet exist in any dictionary, which is part of its usefulness. Locomprotion: local co-promotion, practiced as an art rather than a strategy, pursued with enough imagination and mutual affection that it tips from commerce into something closer to culture. A little loco. Deliberately local. Promotional in the way that a good story is promotional — not by selling but by making the listener want to be there.

This paper is about that word, what it describes, what it could have prevented, what it might still produce, and why a small town in Logan County, Kentucky is a reasonable place to begin finding out.♫ i ♫

I. The Tinker's Art

John Donne's Holy Sonnet XIV — "Batter my heart, three-person'd God" — contains an image that most readers pass over in favor of the poem's more dramatic metaphors of siege and ravishment. The speaker compares himself to a town whose mayor has surrendered it to the enemy and begs God to batter, break, blow, and burn him into genuine fidelity. But nestled inside this violence is a quieter figure: the tinker. He mends. He works with what is already there — the cracked pot, the leaking vessel, the thing that was made well once and has come apart through use and time and neglect. He does not smelt new metal or redesign the object. He restores function, carefully, with the particular attentiveness of someone who respects the original maker's intention.1

The farmer who knows his combine's particular sound well enough to diagnose a problem by ear is practicing the same art. So is the man who keeps a tractor running past the point any dealership would recommend, because he knows its specific disposition and the land it has worked and neither is interchangeable with another. Robert Pirsig called this quality — the attentiveness that knows the particular thing rather than the category — the difference between a motorcycle that is understood and a motorcycle that is merely operated. Logan County has always known the difference. It knows it in its fields and its equipment sheds and its kitchens. The question is whether it knows it in its Main Streets.2♫ ii ♫

The blacksmith's art — breaking and reforging — belongs to forces larger than a working paper. The tinker's art is what is available to the rest of us, and it is not a small thing. Locomprotion is a tinker's art. It does not propose to rebuild rural American economic life from first principles. It proposes to mend the connective tissue between things that are already there — the coffee shop, the gift store, the local artist, the farmers' market, the congregation, the school — until they function as a system rather than as isolated points of light in the dark.

In Russellville, Kentucky — the Logan County seat, eight miles from Lewisburg, an hour north of Nashville and an hour south of Bowling Green — there is a coffee shop called The Flying Pig and a gift and lifestyle shop called The Robyn's Nest. The Flying Pig hosts paint-ins: local artists working in community, the finished work staying in the community, hanging on walls, getting talked about in ways that a concert evaporates and a painting does not. These are not large enterprises. They are exactly the kind of businesses that the economic literature on rural decline treats as symptoms of a problem rather than seeds of a solution. This paper proposes that the literature has it backwards.8

II. What Was Lost, and When

Note on Method The following section engages in clearly labeled speculative and counterfactual reasoning — a legitimate intellectual genre when its premises are announced rather than concealed. Historical claims are real; projections from alternate conditions are thought-experiments in service of understanding the present.

Before the Sears Roebuck catalogue — before the railroad made distance irrelevant to commerce, before national brands made local provenance seem provincial — American small towns were, by necessity, locomprotional. The general store stocked the local blacksmith's tools and the local seamstress's thread and mentioned both by name. The church bulletin was the town's media ecosystem. The harvest festival was simultaneously agricultural fair, arts exhibition, and commercial marketplace. These were not utopian arrangements. They coexisted with racial exclusion, economic brutality, and the grinding limitations of a world without antibiotics or indoor plumbing. The loss of what was good in them does not require pretending that everything was good.

But something specific was lost, and the loss is measurable. Between 1900 and 1970, the number of independent retail businesses per capita in rural American communities fell by approximately 60%.3 The consolidation accelerated after 1990 with big-box retail, and again after 2010 with e-commerce. What replaced the local ecosystem was a national extraction economy in which money spent in a rural community flows immediately to corporate headquarters in a distant city. The local multiplier effect dropped from an estimated 0.52 in strong local economies to 0.15 or below in communities dominated by national chains.4

The Local Multiplier — Real Data Studies by the American Independent Business Alliance and Civic Economics consistently find that locally owned businesses recirculate 2–4 times more money within their communities than chain competitors. A dollar spent at The Flying Pig stays in Logan County at roughly three times the rate of a dollar spent at a national chain. Local owners buy local supplies, employ local people, and contribute to local civic life at rates that national chains structurally cannot match.

The difference between a 0.15 and a 0.45 local multiplier, over ten years in a community of 800, represents several million dollars that either stays in the community or leaves it. The number is not the point. The mechanism is.

III. The Chautauqua Ghost

Robert Pirsig opens Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance with a reference to the Chautauqua — the travelling tent culture of the late nineteenth and early twentieth century that brought lectures, music, theatre, and genuine intellectual engagement to rural American communities that the cities had decided didn't deserve such things. The original Chautauqua reached nearly a third of the American population at its peak. Then it was absorbed. It became a brand. The Chautauqua Institution in New York state still exists and costs several hundred dollars a week to attend. The travelling tent that came to Lewisburg does not exist.5

What replaced it, eventually, was the algorithm. Each successive technology promised to bring the world to the rural community and each delivered, instead, a managed simulation designed to maximize engagement rather than produce genuine encounter. The rural community did not lose access to culture. It lost access to its own culture — the capacity to generate, promote, and celebrate what is specifically and irreplaceably local — and received in exchange an unlimited supply of content produced elsewhere for consumption everywhere.

Locomprotion is not nostalgia for the Chautauqua tent. It is the recognition that the impulse the Chautauqua served did not disappear when the tent was folded. It surfaces in the decision to open a coffee shop in Lewisburg instead of commuting to Bowling Green. It surfaces every time a local business owner decides, against the apparent logic of the market, that this particular place is worth investing in. The question locomprotion asks is: what happens when those individual decisions find each other?

IV. What Locomprotion Actually Looks Like

The practice is simpler than the theory, which is as it should be. When The Flying Pig puts The Robyn's Nest on its chalkboard, and The Robyn's Nest puts a bag of Flying Pig coffee on its counter with a card, and both of them mention the local artist whose work hangs in both spaces, something has happened that is greater than the sum of its parts. Not because of the marketing reach — the combined social media following of three small businesses in a town of eight hundred is not a large number. But because the gesture communicates something no individual business can communicate alone: this is a place.

A place has a character. It generates mild but genuine curiosity in people who hear about it. It rewards the thirty-minute drive in a way that a single destination does not. The destination economy — in which a business tries to be remarkable enough on its own — is a competition small rural businesses cannot win against well-capitalized urban alternatives. The place economy is a different competition entirely, and it is one that Lewisburg can win against Nashville, because Nashville cannot be Lewisburg.

The arts dimension is not decorative. Local music, visual art made from and about the specific landscape and community, craft traditions with genuine regional roots — these are the primary mechanism by which locomprotion generates what economists call experiential differentiation and what everyone else calls a reason to come back. The region has developed what might be called musical speed bumps: curated listening experiences, shared in person rather than broadcast at distance, designed to slow the ear down and widen its range without ever losing the thread back to what Logan County already loves.9

The spiritual dimension belongs in this list without apology. Logan County has a serious religious culture that is, at its best, exactly what the sociological literature on community resilience identifies as a primary driver of local economic and social health: dense networks of mutual obligation, shared narrative about what the community is worth, regular gathering not organized by consumption. A locomprotional ecosystem that excludes this dimension is not describing Logan County.♫ iii ♫

"The destination economy is a competition small towns cannot win.
The place economy is a competition they cannot lose —
if they choose to enter it together."

V. What Twenty Years Could Look Like

Speculative Projection — Clearly Labeled What follows applies documented economic correlations to conditions that do not yet fully exist. Thought-experiments with a mechanism, not forecasts with a guarantee.

Communities with strong local business ecosystems show measurably higher civic participation, lower outmigration among young adults, higher median incomes, and lower rates of the social pathologies associated with economic despair.6

Twenty years of deliberate locomprotional development in a community like Lewisburg — modestly funded, seriously practiced, connected to regional networks — could plausibly produce: a local business ecosystem retaining two to three times more economic value within the community. A cultural identity strong enough to attract the specific kind of visitor looking for exactly what Lewisburg offers. And a model replicable by any rural community with one functioning coffee shop, one functioning gift store, one local artist, and the willingness to begin.

What Outside Investment Actually Funds Infrastructure investment without cultural investment produces ghost towns with better roads. Communities that retain young adults and generate genuine vitality do so because they have something — a character, an identity, a reason to be there beyond employment.

The Flying Pig and The Robyn's Nest are not anchor institutions waiting for outside validation. They are the thing itself, already underway. Outside investment can accelerate but cannot create what they have already started.

VI. The Skin-in-the-Game Principle

Outside investment follows inside commitment. The Mondragon cooperative in the Basque region of Spain — now one of the largest worker-owned economic networks in the world — began in 1956 when a Catholic priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta persuaded five workers to invest their own savings in a cooperative factory. The outside investment that eventually scaled the model arrived because the inside commitment was already visible and credible.7

Medieval craft guilds were funded by the mutual commitment of craftspeople who decided their collective reputation was worth more than their individual competition. The guild mark was a guarantee made by a community to everyone who encountered its products. Outside trust followed inside accountability.♫ iv ♫

In Lewisburg in 2026, the equivalent is modest and specific: local businesses deciding to promote each other as a regular practice, not as a favor but as a conviction. The Flying Pig mentioning The Robyn's Nest. The Robyn's Nest stocking Flying Pig coffee. Both showing up to each other's events. Both telling the story of Lewisburg as a place rather than the story of their individual businesses as destinations.

This is the skin in the game that makes outside investment legible. Not a business plan. Not a grant application. The visible, repeated, public decision that this place is worth promoting — made by the people who have the most to lose if it isn't.

✦ ✦ ✦

VII. Why This, and Why Here

Lewisburg is not a special case. It is a representative case — one of thousands of small American communities with enough latent cultural and economic vitality to sustain a locomprotional ecosystem, and not enough institutional support or cultural self-confidence to have developed one yet. The argument applies to any rural community with a functioning local business presence, a local arts tradition however modest, and at least one institution willing to participate in the mutual promotion of what makes that particular place worth being.

What makes Russellville and Lewisburg a reasonable place to begin is not that they are exceptional but that they have already begun. The Flying Pig exists. The Robyn's Nest exists. WRUS carries Logan County's voice across the region. The Logan Journal carries its stories. The tinker's art requires tools, time, and the conviction that what is being mended is worth mending. The first two can be provided from outside. The third cannot. In this county, the third is already present.

John Donne asked to be battered into faithfulness because gentle persuasion had not been sufficient.
Most small towns do not need battering. They need someone to notice what is already there
and say so clearly enough that the town itself begins to believe it.

This is that attempt.♫ v ♫

Musical Notefoots

Musical notefoots are not footnotes in the conventional sense, as they provide no scholarly apparatus and advance no argument. They are speed bumps — invitations to stop, listen, and return. They are a Lewisburg perpetration associated with Prayer Group X. They are called notefoots because musical notes have feet too, and because the word "footnote" had already been taken. Each link opens in a new tab. The document will wait.

♫ i ♫ FootFoot — The Shaggs (1969). A song about a cat, recorded by three sisters in New Hampshire whose father pulled them out of school to form a band because a fortune teller told him to. Frank Zappa called them his favorite group. They are the best possible introduction to the rest of this document, and to locomprotion generally. The particular thing. Noticed. Sung.
♫ ii ♫ Oh Death — Nimrod Workman (1983). A coal miner from Mercer County, West Virginia, who spent decades underground paying attention to one thing. This is what quality attention sounds like in a human voice. Pirsig would have recognized it immediately. So will Logan County.
♫ iii ♫ Glory in the Meeting House — Apollo's Fire. Sacred Harp tradition, performed with the clarity of people who have decided their collective voice is worth more than their individual silence. This is the sound of inside commitment becoming outside trust. The guild mark, sung.
♫ iv ♫ Bonaparte's Retreat — William H. Stepp (1937). A fiddle tune recorded in Kentucky before the war by one man who had never heard of Aaron Copland. Copland later arranged it into Rodeo, which became canonical. The original is better. This is the journey from local to lasting that locomprotion argues is possible, played on a front porch, preserved on wire.
♫ v ♫ 30 Seconds of Silence: Selah. The final musical notefoot is thirty seconds of silence. This is not a joke, though it is also a little funny. After the tinker's argument, after Donne, after everything — thirty seconds in which nothing is managed, nothing is administered, nothing is delivered. The document ends. The silence begins.
